What is this?

I build experiments in an attempt to learn about human-computer interaction, and uncover more of those laws. I make the experiments available for anyone to participate in, my conclusions available for anyone to read and critique, and I make the raw data available for anyone to crunch.

These experiments also serve as a storehouse of different interfaces that I've seen, admired, or asked questions about.

What do you mean by “experiments?”

We have basic laws of mouse-based actions (like Fitts’ Law and the Accot-Zhai Steering Law), and laws of choice (like the Hick-Hymen Law). These laws tell us things that usually seem obvious in retrospect, but they attach that common sense to real measurements, and give us good back-of-the-envelope guesses about what variables are most important.

laws of action

These laws hardly seem to cover the broad spectrum of interfaces afforded by combinations of mouse, keyboard, touchscreen and stylus. I get ideas for all kinds of strange human-computer interfaces; and (to judge from the movies) I'm not the only one. There's already a preponderance of existing interface elements. How do we choose? What makes one better than another? What variables will alter efficiency and accuracy? These are the questions I want to answer.

This doesn't seem like a good way to measure intuitive, elegant UI.

You're right — it's not.

Because it is in fashion at the moment to aim for a massive audience, of millions or even hundreds of millions of users, much emphasis has been placed on discoverability and intuitive design. That is good! There is a lot of software that everyone should be able to use without a learning curve.

For the most part, intuitive just means “familiar.” If you want to build an intuitive UI, use whatever idioms your audience already uses most frequently.

But I don't work at a startup; I work at a consultancy. We rarely get asked to build a new Facebook or another Twitter. We're more frequently tasked with building software that a small number of people use all day, every day. These are people who have little interest in intuitive design; they want speed and power. They're power users; or they want to be; or their employers want them to be.

Data never tells the whole story; and efficency is never the only goal. But they're certainly part of the story, and part of the goal.

Why? Don't we already know a lot of this?

Perhaps less than you might think; and, if I rediscover something everyone else already knows, I've still learned something.

I'm an Emacs user. When I first tried Sublime Text, it's file navigation was a complete revelation. If you're unfamiliar with it, you should give it a try. In particular, you can search for files based on 'initialisms': with the keystrokes Ctrl-p d a f, I could find the file 'app/directives/auto-focus'.

little gif of find anything

Where before I had to type most of the filename, now I was using four keystrokes! Four keystrokes is short enough to become muscle memory; I could open a file just by thinking about it.

I returned to emacs-land, and did my best to recreate it; Atom has also tried to copy-cat. But everyone seems to copy the appearance of the interface without copying the important part: the scoring algorithm that puts the right file on top of the list. It's not just search, or even fuzzy search. It is its own distinct animal that deserves separate attention and respect.

Is any of this any use to normal people?

I designed an interface for a group of clothing specialists, once. They needed to enter specific, structured data about individual pieces of clothing, and the faster they did, the stronger their business would be.

When I told them they should be using hotkeys, they were terrified. They were not technophilic; they were clothing-people, not computer-people, and they fully expected to be lost trying to use the new software.

But when the new software was finished, I explained it to each of them, and made sure that mnemonics were clearly displayed. And within one workday &mdsash; faster than I had dreamed — 90% of their interactions were keyboard-only. And they loved it.

Many, many jobs now involve using just one or two pieces of specialized software all day. Factory workers, sales associates, warehouse workers, middle managers, delivery truck drivers — really and truly, almost every job involves one piece of software so constant that it becomes almost invisible. It is those interfaces that deserve to be stunningly efficient and fantastically in-tune with their users.
